Can the Numbers Travel?

A proof of concept for evaluating strikers across leagues — not by comparing raw numbers, but by isolating how each player performs specifically in situations that match the difficulty of the target league.

The Problem With Raw Numbers

Cross-league comparisons are everywhere in football. A striker scores 22 goals in Liga Portugal — does that translate? The usual answer is a shrug, maybe a vague "the league is weaker." That's not wrong, but it's not very useful either.

What makes a league harder isn't just the general quality of opposition — it's the specific conditions a striker faces at the moment of action. How quickly defenders close. How tight the grid is when the ball arrives. How elite the aerial opponents are. A player can post impressive numbers in a weaker league, but if those numbers came entirely in situations where he had time and space, then the question of "how would he cope in the Premier League?" stays unanswered.

That's what this system tries to address. Not a general comparison — a difficulty-matched one.

The Idea

The system takes strikers from three source leagues — Eredivisie, Liga Portugal, and Bundesliga — and evaluates them against three target leagues: the Premier League, La Liga, and Bundesliga. But it doesn't compare their season totals. Instead, for each target league, it computes difficulty thresholds from that league's own striker population: what counts as a crowded receipt in the Premier League, what qualifies as a hard shot in La Liga, who is an elite aerial opponent in the Bundesliga.

Then it filters each source player's events down to only the situations that clear those thresholds — the moments in their own league where conditions matched the demands of the target league. The score reflects how they performed in those specific moments only.

The core principle: if a player from Liga Portugal faced 41 receipts where the second-nearest defender was within the Premier League's crowded-situation threshold, and he succeeded in 87% of them — that tells us something more grounded than his overall reception rate.

Three Metrics

The system scores each player on three things, each filtered to match the target league's difficulty:

01

Reception Under Pressure

Ball receipt situations where the second-nearest defender is within the target league's median crowding distance. Success is defined by the player's next meaningful action — a completed pass, dribble, shot, or shielded ball. Carries are skipped; they're automatic, not decisions.

02

Finishing on Hard Chances

Open-play shots with xG at or below the target league's median xG per shot — the harder half of the shot distribution. Measured as absolute goals minus expected goals. Positive means clinical on difficult opportunities; negative means wasteful.

03

Aerial Duels vs Elite Defenders

Aerial contests against opponents whose HOPS rating (StatsBomb's jumping power metric) meets or exceeds the target league's median. Win rate in these specific duels only — not overall aerial figures.

Each metric is scored as a percentile rank within the target league's own striker population. Metrics with too small a sample are excluded from the score rather than zeroed — redistribution is fairer than penalising a player for not having enough hard chances in the filtered subset. The final score is an equal-weighted average across qualifying metrics.

What the Data Returned

Running this across 123 strikers and three target leagues produced 327 evaluations. A few things stood out. Harry Kane, who played in the Bundesliga in 2024/25, scores 94 for the Premier League — the system's top score for that target league. That's a reasonable sanity check: a striker of Kane's quality should rank highly even when the comparison is tightened to PL-difficulty situations.

What This Can't Tell You

This score is descriptive, not predictive. A high score doesn't mean a player will succeed if transferred — it means his performance in situations matching the target league's difficulty looks good relative to that league's strikers. That's a narrower claim, and it should stay that way.

The system can't account for tactical fit, injury history, age trajectory, or the psychological adjustment that comes with moving clubs and countries. It doesn't know that a player's crowded receipts in Eredivisie happened mostly in late-stage cup games against weaker sides. Sample sizes — which the system tracks and reports — can still be small enough to be noisy even when above the minimum threshold.

What it can do is narrow the field in a more principled way than raw goals or general league-difficulty discounts.

Where This Could Go

This is a proof of concept. The data pipeline works, the scoring logic is sound, and the outputs are interpretable — but applying this properly at scale would need a few things this version doesn't have: larger sample coverage across more source leagues, multiple seasons to smooth small-sample noise, and position-specific difficulty thresholds rather than one set for all strikers.

With those additions, a tool like this becomes genuinely useful in a scouting context: not as a final answer, but as a first filter that's better than what most people are doing today.
